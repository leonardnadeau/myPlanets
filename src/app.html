The Solar System

Sun

The Sun is a massive ball of hot plasma at the center of our solar system. It provides the light and heat necessary for life on Earth. Composed mostly of hydrogen and helium, it generates energy through nuclear fusion, converting hydrogen into helium in its core. About 1.4 million kilometers wide, it contains more than 99.8% of the solar system’s total mass.

Mercury

Mercury is the smallest and innermost planet in the solar system. It has a rocky surface full of craters and experiences extreme temperature variations—from scorching hot in the daytime to freezing cold at night. With virtually no atmosphere to retain heat, Mercury orbits the Sun in just 88 Earth days, making it the fastest planet.

Venus

Current orbit speed : ...km/h

Venus, often called Earth’s twin because of its similar size, is covered by thick clouds of carbon dioxide and sulfuric acid. This dense atmosphere traps heat in a runaway greenhouse effect, making Venus the hottest planet in the solar system. Its surface pressure is 90 times that of Earth’s, and it rotates backward compared to most planets.

Earth

Current orbit speed : ...km/h

Earth is the only known planet to support life, thanks to its liquid water, breathable atmosphere, and moderate temperatures. About 71% of its surface is covered by oceans. The planet has one natural satellite—the Moon—and an atmosphere that protects life from harmful solar radiation while regulating temperature.

Moon

Current orbit speed : ...km/h

Earth’s Moon is our closest celestial neighbor and the only natural satellite of our planet. It orbits Earth once every 27.3 days and influences ocean tides through its gravitational pull. The Moon’s surface is covered in craters and ancient volcanic plains called maria. Its formation likely resulted from a giant impact between early Earth and a Mars-sized body.

Mars

Current orbit speed : ...km/h

Mars, the Red Planet, is known for its rusty surface caused by iron oxide dust. It has the largest volcano in the solar system, Olympus Mons, and deep valleys like Valles Marineris. Mars once had flowing water and may have conditions suitable for microbial life. It’s a key target for future human exploration.

Jupiter

Current orbit speed : ...km/h

Jupiter is the largest planet in the solar system, a gas giant made mostly of hydrogen and helium. Its most recognizable feature is the Great Red Spot—a massive storm larger than Earth that has lasted for centuries. Jupiter’s strong magnetic field and more than 90 moons, including Ganymede (the largest in the solar system), make it a fascinating world of extremes.

Saturn

Current orbit speed : ...km/h

Saturn is famous for its stunning ring system, composed of ice and rock particles. Like Jupiter, it’s a gas giant mostly made of hydrogen and helium. Saturn has over 140 moons, with Titan being the largest and having a dense atmosphere rich in methane. Despite its massive size, Saturn is the least dense planet—it could float in water if a large enough ocean existed.

Uranus

Current orbit speed : ...km/h

Uranus is an ice giant with a pale blue color caused by methane in its atmosphere. Uniquely, it rotates on its side, possibly due to a massive collision in the past. Its unusual tilt causes extreme seasons lasting over 20 years each. Uranus has faint rings and at least 27 known moons.

Neptune

Current orbit speed : ...km/h

Neptune is the farthest planet from the Sun and another ice giant. It has strong winds and storms, including the Great Dark Spot—similar to Jupiter’s storm systems. Its deep blue color comes from methane and unknown atmospheric components. Neptune’s largest moon, Triton, orbits in the opposite direction of the planet’s rotation, hinting it was captured long ago.
